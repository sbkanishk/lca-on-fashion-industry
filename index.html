Historical Context

How We Got Here — A Timeline

Fashion's environmental impact didn't happen overnight. Here's how the industry evolved from slow craft to planetary crisis.

1800s–1950s
Slow Fashion Era
Clothing was expensive, local, and made to last. Families owned few garments and repaired them routinely. The average American bought 3 garments per year. Environmental impact was minimal and localised.
~3 garments/person/year
1960s–1980s
Synthetic Fibre Revolution
Polyester, nylon, and acrylic — petroleum-based fibres — transformed the industry. Clothing became cheaper and more disposable. Global cotton demand surged, triggering mass irrigation and the beginning of the Aral Sea's collapse.
Aral Sea: lost 90% volume by 2007
1990s
Globalisation & Outsourcing
Production shifted to low-wage countries (Bangladesh, China, Cambodia). Supply chains lengthened dramatically, increasing transport emissions. Labour and environmental standards weakened. Brands like Zara pioneered short production cycles.
85% of garments now made in developing nations
2000s
Fast Fashion Explosion
Zara, H&M, and Primark introduced the "52 seasons" model — new collections weekly. Global clothing production doubled between 2000 and 2015. Consumers in the EU now buy 60% more clothing than 15 years ago, but keep each garment half as long.
Production doubled; garment lifespan halved
2013
Rana Plaza Collapse
A garment factory in Bangladesh collapsed, killing 1,134 workers. The disaster sparked global awareness of the true human and environmental cost of fast fashion.
1,134 lives lost · turning point for transparency
2015–2020
Sustainability Reckoning
Fashion emerged as the world's 2nd or 3rd most polluting industry. The UN Alliance for Sustainable Fashion was formed. First mandatory EPR laws appeared in France.
Fashion = 10% of global CO₂, 20% of water pollution
2020–Now
The Circular Economy Push
The EU Green Deal targets mandatory sustainability labelling by 2026. Resale platforms (Vinted, ThredUp) grew 20× faster than retail. Bio-based fibres, waterless dyeing, and fibre-to-fibre recycling reached commercial scale.
Resale market to exceed fast fashion by 2030

Take Action

How You Can Reduce the Impact

The fashion industry's footprint is enormous — but most of the power lies with consumers. Small, deliberate changes across buying, care, and disposal habits can reduce a garment's lifetime impact by over 60%.

🌿
Choose organic or natural fibres

Organic cotton uses 91% less water and eliminates synthetic pesticides. Linen and hemp need minimal water with no pesticides at all.

↓ 60–91% water saved
♻️
Buy recycled synthetics

Recycled polyester (rPET) uses 59% less energy and emits 75% less CO₂ than virgin polyester — without sacrificing performance.

↓ 75% CO₂ reduction
🕰
Buy less, wear longer

Doubling the lifespan of a garment from 1 year to 2 reduces its total climate impact by 24%. Wearing it 3× longer cuts impact by ~65%.

↓ 65% lifetime impact
👗
Buy secondhand first

Secondhand clothing has already absorbed its production impact. Buying pre-loved avoids all raw material extraction and manufacturing emissions.

↓ ~80% production impact
🏷
Check certifications

Look for GOTS (organic textile), OEKO-TEX (chemical safety), Fair Trade, or bluesign labels — these verify responsible production along the supply chain.

Verified supply chain
🚫
Avoid fast fashion hauls

Fast fashion brands release 52 "micro-seasons" per year. Each garment has a shorter lifespan, amplifying per-use impact and waste generation significantly.

↓ reduces waste by 40%
🌊
Wash in cold water

90% of the energy used in a washing machine goes toward heating water. Cold washing saves up to 0.6 kg CO₂ per cycle with no reduction in cleaning performance.

↓ 90% washing energy
💨
Air-dry instead of tumble

Tumble dryers are the most energy-intensive home appliance per cycle. Air drying eliminates this entirely — and extends garment lifespan by reducing fibre stress.

↓ 100% dryer energy
🔁
Wash less often

Most clothes don't need washing after every wear. Spot-clean where needed. Washing jeans every 10 wears instead of every wear reduces their water impact by 75%.

↓ 75% water per garment
🔬
Use a microfibre filter bag

Synthetic fabrics shed 500,000+ microfibres per wash that flow into oceans. Guppyfriend or Cora Ball filter bags catch 90% of these microplastic fibres.

↓ 90% microplastic shed
🧴
Use eco-certified detergent

Conventional detergents contain surfactants, phosphates, and fragrances that persist in waterways. Eco-certified alternatives biodegrade rapidly and reduce aquatic toxicity.

↓ chemical pollution
🪡
Repair before replacing

A simple button reattachment or seam repair extends garment life by years. Learning basic mending skills is one of the highest-impact individual actions available.

Extends life 2–5 years
🤝
Donate to charity shops

Donating clothes in good condition extends their lifecycle and displaces new purchases. This is the single most impactful end-of-life choice — reducing disposal impact by ~70%.

↓ 70% end-of-life impact
📦
Use textile collection bins

Worn-out clothes not suitable for donation can be dropped at textile recycling bins (H&M, Zara, municipality bins). Fibres are recovered for insulation, industrial rags, or new yarn.

↓ 62% waste impact
🛒
Sell on resale platforms

Platforms like Vinted, Depop, and ThredUp let you sell unwanted clothes directly to new owners — earning money while keeping textiles in use and out of landfill.

Keeps textiles in use
🎨
Upcycle into new items

Old jeans become shorts, old T-shirts become tote bags or cleaning rags. Upcycling gives garments a creative second life and requires zero new production energy.

Zero production energy
🚯
Never bin fast fashion

Fast fashion items in landfill take 200+ years to decompose, leaching dye chemicals into groundwater and releasing methane. Even damaged synthetics should go to textile bins.

Avoids 200yr decompose
🔄
Participate in clothing swaps

Community clothing swap events let you exchange garments you no longer wear for ones you will — zero production, zero cost, zero waste.

Zero-waste exchange
💧
Waterless dyeing technology

Supercritical CO₂ dyeing uses pressurised carbon dioxide instead of water — eliminating toxic effluent and reducing energy use by 50%. Already adopted by Nike and DyeCoo.

↓ 100% dye effluent
☀️
Renewable energy in factories

Switching manufacturing facilities from coal/gas to solar and wind power can cut production-stage CO₂ emissions by over 60%.

↓ 60% factory CO₂
🔬
Bio-based & circular fibres

Innovations like Mylo (mushroom leather), Bolt Threads' spider silk, and Piñatex (pineapple fibre) replace petroleum-based synthetics with biodegradable alternatives.

Biodegradable materials
📋
Extended Producer Responsibility

Legislation that makes brands financially responsible for their products' end-of-life. France's EPR law (2021) requires brands to fund textile collection and recycling infrastructure.

Systemic policy change
🏷
Mandatory LCA labelling

Requiring brands to disclose water use, CO₂ emissions, and chemical inputs per garment at point of sale — proposed in the EU by 2026.

Consumer transparency
⚖️
Tax on fast fashion

A proposed UK parliamentary bill (2019) would impose a 1p tax per garment to fund textile recycling. Similar taxes are under review across the EU, Australia, and Canada.

Economic incentive
